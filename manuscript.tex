\Title{Wind Does Not Disrupt Overwintering Monarch Butterfly Clusters: Direct Empirical Test of a Three-Decade Management Assumption}

%% ------------------------------------------------------------------
%% Simple Summary (required by Insects)
%% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\simplesumm{The population size of monarch butterfly in the western U.S. has been declining since the 1980s. This population spends the winter months in groves of trees along the California coast. Habitat management in these groves has focused on controlling wind, as wind we start to have a negative affect. Though that has seemed like a good idea and the best approach, it is not until now that the fundamental assumption of wind needing to be controlled has finally been tested. We find that wind itself does not have any negative effects and therefore does not need to be controlled. Instead, we find that sunlight and wind interact to warm and cool monarch butterflies. This new finding will change the way in which we manage groves. Instead of managing or suppressing wind, combinations of wind and light should be provided within the groves, so that monarch butterflies can chose among suitable thermal conditions.}

%% ------------------------------------------------------------------
%% Abstract
%% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\abstract{The western monarch butterfly (\textit{Danaus plexippus}) population has declined by over 99\% since the 1980s, making conservation of overwintering habitat along California's coast a management priority. For three decades, the primary perspective on overwintering habitat has utilized a Microclimate Hypothesis which posits monarch butterflies seek a narrow microclimate for overwintering, including a nested Disruptive Wind Hypothesis which posits that wind speeds exceeding 2~m/s disrupt butterfly clusters and cause roost abandonment. In earlier work, we tested and rejected the Microclimate Hypothesis for temperature, humidity and light, but did not address wind. This study provides the first direct experimental test of wind effects on overwintering monarch clusters.

We deployed cameras and wind sensors at two sites on California's Vandenberg Space Force Base, during the 2023--2024 overwintering season. Time-lapse photography, documenting changes in butterfly abundance, and wind sensors adjacent to roosts, simultaneously collected 1,894 paired observations at 30-minute intervals across 78 days. Linear regression analysis revealed a poor relationship between wind speed and changes in cluster size at either 30-minute or daily timescales ($R^{2} = 0.002$, $p = 0.073$ and $R^{2} = 0.013$, $p = 0.252$, respectively). Generalized additive mixed models identified temperature, time of day, and an interaction between wind and sunlight as factors influencing cluster behavior ($p < 0.001$). Wind alone showed no independent effect. Instead, wind effects appeared to be light dependent: when butterflies remained in shade, wind speed had no effect on cluster behavior (up to 12.4~m/s). Effects appeared, and the number of clustering butterflies changed, only when butterflies occupied sunlit positions.

Our findings challenge a fundamental assumption in monarch habitat management. Instead of managing for controlled wind speeds, our results indicate that both light and wind contribute to clustering decisions. Thus, we suggest that thermoregulation may be a primary driver of clustering behavior. This suggestion is consistent with previous work on thermal physiology of overwintering Western monarch butterflies. Thermal ecology should provide a new perspective for science-based management of overwintering habitat in California.}

\section{Introduction}
%% ------------------------------------------------------------------

The distribution and survival of invertebrate species are governed by complex interplays of biotic and abiotic factors. While biotic interactions influence community assembly in part through predation, competition, and parasitism \cite{blois-heulinDirectIndirectEffects1990,laffertyComparingMechanismsHost2013,miller-terkuilePredatorPreyInteractions2022}, abiotic conditions often set the fundamental limits determining where invertebrates can persist \cite{sextonEvolutionEcologySpecies2009,louthanWhereWhenSpecies2015,thomasClimateChangeRange2010}. Temperature constrains invertebrate physiology across all latitudes, from Antarctic midges \cite{everattResponsesInvertebratesTemperature2015} to desert land snails \cite{schweizerSnailsSunStrategies2019}. In invertebrates, temperature correlates positively with metabolic rates and respiratory water loss, creating compound stresses that limit activity windows \cite{chownWaterLossInsects2011}. In addition, via a correlation with temperature, solar radiation can drive both direct physiological impacts and behavioral responses. Examples range from army ants demonstrating habitat-specific thermal maxima tied to insolation exposure \cite{baudierExtremeInsolationClimatic2018}, to terrestrial gastropods climbing vertical surfaces to escape ground-level heat \cite{schweizerSnailsSunStrategies2019}.

Wind is another abiotic factor that can influence invertebrate distribution and behavior through diverse mechanisms that vary across species. There are remarkably specific wind detection thresholds and behavioral responses. For example, cockroaches perceive air currents as low as 0.015--0.03~m/s and demonstrate predictable orientation shifts in response to wind velocities \cite{bellSearchAnemotacticOrientation1979}. Mosquitoes' sensitivity to winds as light as 1.0~m/s can effectively ground entire populations \cite{bidlingmayerEffectWindVelocity1995}. Some species exploit wind for dispersal through specific behaviors, such as spiders' stereotypic ``tiptoe'' behavior which initiates ballooning but only when winds permit controlled trajectories \cite{bonteHeritabilitySpiderBallooning2007}. Some species respond to wind as an environmental stressor requiring active avoidance. For mad hatterpillar larvae, 3~m/s winds are a disturbance cue, triggering movements to stable microsites on branches, and behavioral shifts to leeward sides and abaxial leaf surfaces \cite{leonardExposureWindAlters2016}. Underlying many of these behavioral avoidance responses are the fundamental thermal and hydric effects of wind, as convective cooling from air movement significantly alters both heat and water exchanges between organisms and their environment, particularly when wind interacts with solar radiation \cite{riddellWindNicheThermal2025}. Thus, wind can be recognized as having direct mechanical effects, and indirect thermal and hydric effects through covariates such as heat and insolation.

The monarch butterfly (\textit{Danaus plexippus}) offers an exceptional system for studying how abiotic factors shape invertebrate ecology, and much is known about this migratory species. Late summer environmental cues, such as shortening day length and cooling temperatures, trigger the emergence of a migratory monarch generation \cite{reppertDemystifyingMonarchButterfly2018,goehringEffectsPhotoperiodTemperature2002,hermanJuvenileHormoneRegulation2001,barkerEffectPhotoperiodTemperature1976}. These adults exhibit unique characteristics including reproductive diapause (suspended reproduction), and an elongated-wing long-travel-distance phenotype \cite{barkerEffectPhotoperiodTemperature1976,yangIntrapopulationVariationNatal2016,tuskesOverwinteringEcologyMonarch1978}. The fall migrants collect nectar enroute thus building lipid reserves needed for overwintering \cite{hermanJuvenileHormoneRegulation2001,chaplinEnergyReservesMetabolic1982,Urquhart1978Autumnal}. And while Eastern monarchs travel to high-altitude oyamel fir (\textit{Abies religiosa}) forests in central Mexico, our focus, the Western monarch population, migrates to overwintering groves widely scattered along the Pacific coast of California \cite{browerUnderstandingMisunderstandingMigration1995,Urquhart1978Autumnal}.

While overwintering, roughly from mid-October through mid-March, monarchs survive on accumulated lipid reserves and associated thermoregulatory balancing \cite{chaplinEnergyReservesMetabolic1982,mastersMonarchButterflyDanaus1988}. As ectotherms, monarchs face a fundamental trade-off: maintain body temperatures cool enough through metabolic suppression to conserve energy for the entire overwintering season, yet warm enough to permit for bouts of essential flights \cite{mastersMonarchButterflyDanaus1988,chaplinEnergyReservesMetabolic1982,kammerThoracicTemperatureShivering1970,alonso-mejiaInfluenceTemperatureSurface1992}. Temperatures of 12.7--16.0$^{\circ}$C represent the minimum thoracic temperature required for powered flight \cite{mastersMonarchButterflyDanaus1988} and represent flight threshold temperature for (Eastern) monarchs. Below this threshold, monarchs employ behavioral thermoregulation either through dorsal basking, which can elevate body temperature to flight capability within 30 seconds of sun exposure, or through energetically costly shivering that consumes energy 25 times faster than resting \cite{mastersMonarchButterflyDanaus1988}. Thus ``warm enough'' can be environmentally or physiologically attained, but at significantly different costs to lipid stores. Conversely, direct sun exposure can rapidly elevate thoracic temperatures to 33.6$^{\circ}$C, forcing butterflies to adopt sun-minimizing postures or engage in heat-dissipating flights \cite{barkerEffectPhotoperiodTemperature1976}. This energetic balancing means that monarchs selecting overwintering sites must find locations that minimize both sustained freezing exposure and elevated metabolic expenditure \cite{mastersMonarchButterflyDanaus1988,alonso-mejiaInfluenceTemperatureSurface1992,calvertEffectRainSnow1983,andersonFreezeprotectionOverwinteringMonarch1996}.

Our focus on abiotic conditions, but more specifically wind, builds on early studies from Mexican overwintering sites that lead Leong \cite{leongMicroenvironmentalFactorsAssociated1990} to propose the Microclimate Hypothesis for western monarchs overwintering in California. This hypothesis posits that monarchs select clustering locations within groves based on a measurable microenvironmental envelope characterized by four key parameters each with a specific benefit: wind protection or speeds below 2~m/s ($\sim$5~mph) preventing cluster disruption, cool temperatures maintaining diapause while minimizing freezing risk and elevated metabolic expenditure, dappled sunlight enabling behavioral thermoregulation, and high humidity or accessible moisture to prevent desiccation \cite{leongMicroenvironmentalFactorsAssociated1990,leongUseMultivariateAnalyses1991}. Wind emerged as particularly critical in Leong's Microclimate Hypothesis.

Leong's initial studies measured wind speeds at trees with and without butterfly clusters, concluding that ``during winter storms, the butterflies clustered on those few trees that offered the greatest protection against winds of 2~m/s or greater'' \cite{leongMicroenvironmentalFactorsAssociated1990}. Though, technically the highest wind speed measured in this study was 1.66~m/s. Based on these inferred correlations Leong reported that ``butterflies cluster in areas of the grove that offer exposure to filtered sunlight and shelter from strong wind movement'' \cite{leongUseMultivariateAnalyses1991}. In later publications, Leong added anecdotal observations, noting that ``roosting butterflies were blown from their clusters or dislodged by excessive branch movements (shaking) caused by winds exceeding 2~m/s'' and that ``upon dislodgment, the butterflies flew to and resettled on foliage of trees that offered better shelter from strong winds'' \cite{leongRestorationOverwinteringGrove1999}. When storm winds coincided with morning temperatures below 15$^{\circ}$C, ``the dislodged butterflies would be blown to the ground and remain there until temperatures reached flight threshold,'' with ``butterflies littering the ground at the base of roosting trees'' commonly observed after winter storms \cite{leongRestorationOverwinteringGrove1999}. Though the Microclimate Hypothesis originated from untested anecdotal observations, it still gained widespread acceptance and came to directly inform management guidelines at multiple levels. Leong's 2016 synthesis for California Department of Fish and Wildlife formally established that ``winds $\geq$ 2~m/s are disruptive to the aggregating butterflies by blowing them from their roosting branches or dislodging them by shaking the branches,'' and that when subjected to such winds when above flight threshold temperature, butterflies ``either flew to a more sheltered area of the grove or, if no refuge area was available, abandoned the grove temporarily or for the remainder of the season'' \cite{leongEvaluationManagementCalifornia2016}. Thus, the Microclimate Hypothesis ``made obvious'' that successful overwintering sites would consistently exhibit a specific suite of conditions, including protection from wind $\geq$ 2~m/s. This then implied that specific abiotic parameters should guide habitat management and restoration efforts, a principle now widely adopted in monarch conservation plans \cite{xercessocietyStateMonarchOverwintering2016,jepsenProtectingCaliforniasButterfly2017,peltonMonarchButterflyOverwintering2020,weissRecommendationsRestorationMonarch2008,althouse&meadeinc.EllwoodMesaSperling2023}.

However, empirical testing has gradually dismantled the Microclimate Hypothesis' core assumption of a uniform environmental envelope. When researchers tested whether monarchs select for consistent microclimatic attributes (temperature, light, and humidity) across groves, they found that the realized microclimate varied significantly with latitude and local geography \cite{sanieeHierarchyScaleInfluence2022}. Temperature responses proved more complex than originally proposed, with monarchs avoiding freezing temperatures but selecting the warmest cold temperatures available at their latitude \cite{fisherMultiScaleSpeciesDistribution2024}
. Humidity showed geographic variability rather than consistency \cite{sanieeHierarchyScaleInfluence2022}. Of the three Microclimate Hypothesis parameters tested, only light exposure showed potential support as a unifying factor, with successful overwintering sites exhibiting consistent patterns of canopy openness \cite{weissForestCanopyStructure1991} and resultant high intensity light of short duration \cite{sanieeHierarchyScaleInfluence2022}, or specific shading patterns \cite{ibsenDensityDependenceHabitat2026}
. This geographic variability in microclimate utilization, where selection occurs hierarchically across multiple spatial scales rather than for a single environmental envelope, fundamentally contradicts Leong's original hypothesis \cite{fisherMultiScaleSpeciesDistribution2024}
\cite{sanieeHierarchyScaleInfluence2022}.

Among the four parameters of Leong's Microclimate Hypothesis, the wind disruption component of the Microclimate Hypothesis (hereafter Wind Disruption Hypothesis) stands apart as the only major component that has never undergone direct empirical testing of butterfly behavioral responses. Despite widespread adoption of the Wind Disruption Hypothesis' 2~m/s wind protection threshold in restoration efforts \cite{xercessocietyManagingMonarchsWest2018,jepsenProtectingCaliforniasButterfly2017,althouse&meadeinc.EllwoodMesaSperling2023,u.s.fishandwildlifeserviceMonarchDanausPlexippus2024,weissAlbanyHillMonarch2018,leongEvaluationManagementCalifornia2016}, no study has established causal relationships between wind exposure and butterfly behavior. Leong's methodology of comparing wind measurements between occupied and unoccupied trees provided only correlational evidence, never directly testing butterfly responses to wind exposure, or distinguishing whether butterflies actively avoid wind, hydric or thermic effects of wind, or some other correlated unmeasured variables.

The absence of empirical testing for the Wind Disruption Hypothesis becomes critically important given the catastrophic decline of western monarch populations. Between the 1980s and mid-2010s, populations collapsed by approximately 97\% \cite{schultzCitizenScienceMonitoring2017}, with population viability analyses revealing a 72\% quasi-extinction risk within 20 years \cite{schultzCitizenScienceMonitoring2017}. The winter 2018 to 2019 count recorded fewer than 30,000 monarchs, a single-year drop of 86\%, representing over 99\% decline from 1980s abundance \cite{peltonWesternMonarchPopulation2019}. The situation reached its most critical state in 2020 when the Western Monarch Thanksgiving Count documented only 1,901 butterflies, the lowest number ever recorded \cite{xercessocietyWesternMonarchButterfly2025}. While populations showed some recovery in subsequent years, the 2024 to 2025 count of 9,119 represents the second-lowest count and the latest count of 12,260 (2025--2026) is the third lowest on record, demonstrating the population's continued precarity \cite{xercessocietyWesternMonarchButterfly2025}. These dramatic declines result from multiple interacting stressors including herbicide use and associated breeding habitat loss, pesticide exposure, climate change, and the loss and degradation of overwintering habitat \cite{croneWhyAreMonarch2019,peltonWesternMonarchPopulation2019,oberhauser2026MonarchConservation}. Evidence increasingly suggests that the overwintering stage represents the most limiting phase of the monarch's annual cycle, with population declines concentrated during winter and early spring periods \cite{peltonWesternMonarchPopulation2019,mariniDensitydependenceDecliningPopulation2017}. California's coastal overwintering groves, where the entire western population concentrates into small, forested areas from October through March, have thus emerged as the focal point of Western Monarch conservation priorities. Yet management of these critical sites continues to rely on the untested Disruptive Wind Hypothesis, with restoration efforts investing millions of conservation dollars based on the presumed necessity of maintaining wind speeds below 2~m/s \cite{althouse&meadeinc.EllwoodMesaSperling2023,themonarchpressCityAwarded392019}.

Testing the Disruptive Wind Hypothesis requires addressing a fundamental methodological challenge: isolating wind effects from confounding environmental variables that naturally covary in field settings. Solar radiation is a potential covariate that can trigger butterfly departures through direct heating \cite{mastersMonarchButterflyDanaus1988,kammerThoracicTemperatureShivering1970}. Temperature is a potential covariate known to independently influence activity patterns, with monarchs exhibiting predictable responses as temperatures approach and exceed flight thresholds \cite{barkerEffectPhotoperiodTemperature1976}. Time of day creates inherent activity rhythms related to sun angle and thermal conditions \cite{mouritsenVirtualMigrationTethered2002} if clusters reform nightly. Furthermore, wind itself is multidimensional, characterized not only by average speed but also by gustiness and variability \cite{nathanLongdistanceBiologicalTransport2005} that could differentially affect butterfly behavior. A rigorous test must therefore control for potentially confounding factors while examining multiple aspects of wind exposure.

This study provides the first direct empirical test of whether wind disrupts overwintering monarch butterflies. Our primary objective was to evaluate the 2~m/s wind disruption threshold that has guided over three decades of conservation practice. First, we hypothesized that wind, alongside other environmental factors, predicts butterfly abundance at overwintering clusters. If true, we predict that wind will emerge as a significant predictor of abundance changes, with monarch abundance (i.e., cluster size) decreasing when exposed to higher wind speeds. Second, we hypothesized that wind becomes disruptive above a specific threshold of 2~m/s. If this threshold represents a meaningful biological boundary, we predict that monarch abundance (i.e., cluster size) will decline at roosts experiencing winds exceeding 2~m/s. Third, we hypothesized that wind's disruptive effects scale with intensity. If disruption increases with wind speed, we predict proportionally greater decreases in monarch abundance (i.e., cluster size) as wind speeds rise above the threshold. Finally, we hypothesized that experiencing disruptive winds affects monarch site fidelity. If wind disruption influences future roost selection, we predict decreased site fidelity manifested as sustained abundance reductions at wind-disturbed roosts compared to pre-disturbance levels.

\section{Materials and Methods}
%% ------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Study Site}

Vandenberg Space Force Base (VSFB, 34.7398$^{\circ}$N, 120.5725$^{\circ}$W) in Santa Barbara County, California, was selected based on: extensive historical plantings of blue gum eucalyptus (\textit{Eucalyptus globulus}) that support overwintering habitat, historical occupation of that habitat, restricted access providing security for long-term equipment deployment, and federal funding that mandated research be conducted on federal lands. VSFB contains thirty documented overwintering groves, with several over the last decade consistently in the top 10\% statewide based on population abundance \cite{xercessocietyWesternMonarchThanksgiving2025}. Ten locations were selected from the thirty based on consistent occupancy by monarch aggregations, year-round research access, and local knowledge by Jessica Griffiths the VSFB Monarch Conservation Coordinator and long-term VSFB coordinator of Western Monarch Thanksgiving Counts \cite{xercessocietyWesternMonarchThanksgiving2025}. We deployed monitoring equipment at these locations across two overwintering seasons, with only four sites monitored during 2023--2024 and all ten sites during 2024--2025. However, due to low monarch abundance during the first season and no observed overwintering monarchs during the second season, only two sites (Spring Canyon and UDMH in 2023--2024) produced measurable butterfly clusters suitable for our analysis. Spring Canyon (34.6315$^{\circ}$N, 120.6182$^{\circ}$W) is the most historically reliable and highest overwintering abundance site on VSFB. In the South Base and within 300 meters of Space Launch Complex 4, it is approximately 2.0-hectares of mature blue gum eucalyptus (BGU) ranging to 40 meters in height. A perennial creek runs through the center of the overwintering grove, creating a riparian corridor supporting heterogeneous canopy structure, variable tree spacing, and diverse understory vegetation. Surf Road, an infrequently used but paved access road, bisects both the creek and forest canopy. Although only recently (2022) documented, UDMH has emerged as a significant overwintering site, ranking among the base's highest population sites since 2022. UDMH (34.6719$^{\circ}$N, 120.5950$^{\circ}$W), is also located in South Base, comprises 5.1-hectares of BGE windrows planted adjacent to a waste treatment facility, and has an understory of scattered low shrubs.

\subsection{Monitoring Strategy}

The 2023--2024 season employed two strategies: targeted deployments at sites with monarchs present (Spring Canyon and UDMH), and anticipatory deployments based on historical occupancy data but without evident occupancy. Anticipatory deployments occurred at four overwintering groves: additional locations within Spring Canyon and UDMH, plus SLC-6 and Tangair. No monarchs were recorded at anticipatory deployment sites in 2023--2024; consequently, these data are excluded from analysis. Unfortunately, the 2024--2025 season coincided with historically low monarch abundance throughout California \cite{xercessocietyWesternMonarchThanksgiving2025}, thus we do not present deployment strategies, data or analysis for any of the sites monitored in 2024--2025.

\subsection{Field Equipment}

To observe changes in monarch abundance (cluster size) in response to wind, we deployed remote monitoring equipment adjacent to butterfly clusters using 15-meter-tall fiberglass telescoping poles (Max-Gain Systems, Inc., Marietta, GA). Poles were anchored at three points, with ground anchors and guy lines connecting the base (anchors) to the pole's top, thus creating stable freestanding structures. Poles were 4--17 meters (horizontal distance) from their cluster. This distance was determined through field testing to provide the image resolution required by our grid-based counting method (see below). Specifically, this positioning minimized disturbance to monarchs, provided sufficient field of view, and provided necessary resolution of individual clustered butterflies. In addition, pole placement was often constrained by ground stability relative to the freestanding structure, structure footprint, clearance requirements, and viewing angles. Deployments near active clusters did minimize disturbance; no butterfly dispersal was observed during equipment deployment.

Monarch abundance (cluster size) was monitored using trail cameras (GardePro E7 and E8, Shenzhen, China) modified for near-infrared (NIR) imaging which enhanced clustering butterfly and vegetation contrast \cite{hristovEstimatingOverwinteringMonarch2019}, which in turn improved abundance estimation. Cameras were chosen for durability under extended field deployment, native time-lapse functionality, and modification potential. Modifications exploited an internal filter-switching mechanism by engaging the clear glass filter, disconnecting power to prevent reversion to infrared, and mounting NIR pass filters ($>$850~nm) in front of the native lens. Restricting incoming light to NIR wavelengths produced black and white images where clustering butterflies appeared as dark masses against bright eucalyptus foliage, exploiting the high NIR reflectance characteristic of living vegetation \cite{gatesSpectralPropertiesPlants1965,thajAnalysisOpticalProperties2025}. Field comparison to human-labeler (see below) confirmed sufficient visual contrast for accurate estimation of cluster size. Cameras, in time-lapse mode with motion detection disabled, were mounted atop poles and positioned horizontally toward butterfly clusters at roost height. Wireless live-view enabled real-time preview and precise camera aiming at the time of deployment. The camera sampling interval, which balanced information content and temporal resolution, battery life and human-labeler data processing capacity, was determined through statistical validation. Initial deployments used 10-minute intervals to capture images of changing butterfly abundance. Post-deployment analyses using mixed-effects models and information-theoretic approaches systematically conducted sequential subsampling starting with full temporal resolution (10-minute interval) and progressively testing reduced frequencies. Model comparison, using Akaike Information Criterion (AIC), demonstrated that 30-minute intervals provided optimal balance, losing less than 5\% of information (measured by root mean square error) compared to full temporal resolution while reducing image processing workload by 67\%. Variance comparison analysis and visual assessment of fitted trend lines confirmed that this interval preserved essential time-series patterns including diurnal activity cycles, weather-response dynamics, and multi-day population trends. Battery life constraints and field deployment logistics were consistent with this interval, enabling extended autonomous operation essential for capturing complete behavioral sequences during variable weather conditions.

To quantify if predicted wind conditions influenced butterfly behavior, wind monitoring equipment (WindLog Wind Data Loggers, Rain Wise Inc., Trenton, Maine) was installed at pole apices thereby measuring wind at approximate butterfly roost heights. Wind loggers recorded average wind speed and maximum wind gust at one-minute intervals. This was the shortest interval supported by the sensors, enabled calculation of wind speed minimum, maximum, and variance within each 30-minute photographic interval, and captured gustiness otherwise lost with longer intervals.

Monitoring equipment was reused across locations within a season, with repositioning following cluster aggregations. To systematically organize our monitoring efforts, we defined discrete monitoring periods as deployment units. Each deployment unit represented a unique combination of monitoring location, camera configuration (camera ID, mounting height, and viewing angle), associated wind measurements, and photos for abundance estimates, over a temporal period. This deployment-based structure provided standardized sampling units that accounted for variation in environmental conditions and equipment configurations while treating each deployment as an independent time-series.

\subsection{Image Analysis}

\subsubsection{Grid-Based Counting Method for Images}

To systematically quantify changes in overwintering butterfly abundance from deployment images, we developed a grid-based counting protocol that balanced accuracy with the practical constraints of analyzing tens of thousands of images. This approach addresses the challenge presented by large aggregations where counts of all individuals would be prohibitively time-consuming, and where estimates of abundance would be a normal alternative, as is typical in monarch field research (e.g., Thanksgiving Count, \cite{xercessocietyManagingMonarchsWest2018}). But, because images were available, and we were not under field conditions, we could be somewhat more systematic. Thus, we subdivided each image using a grid overlay system whereafter human labelers assigned order-of-magnitude estimates (0: no butterflies, 1--9: single digits, 10--99: dozens, and 100--999: hundreds) per cell. Grid dimensions (and thus number of cells) were optimized per deployment to ensure most occupied cells contained butterflies in the 10--99 count range. Grid dimensions remained fixed within each deployment to ensure consistency in estimates of abundance within a time series. Grid dimensions varied across deployments based on camera-to-cluster distance. This standardization balanced classification efficiency (effort) with spatial resolution, minimized cells randomly/erroneously alternating between different order-of-magnitude categories within a time series, and was facilitated with custom software developed using the Electron framework in JavaScript (\url{https://kylenessen.github.io/monarch_trailcam_classifier/}).

\subsubsection{Counting Protocol}

Human labelers, trained on a comprehensive online guide with example images and detailed classification criteria, estimated butterfly abundance within each grid cell using the four order-of-magnitude categories (\url{https://kylenessen.github.io/monarch_trailcam_classifier/}). The protocol optimized efficiency while maintaining consistency across observers. For example, abundance estimates derived from two-dimensional image planes only, without estimating three-dimensional cluster abundance, structure or depth. This approach intentionally excluded hidden individuals behind visible butterflies in overlapping aggregations, thus providing a conservative and less subjective but more consistent measure of abundance. When cells contained partial butterflies at grid boundaries, labelers included these in counts unless double-counting bumped an adjacent cell into a higher category. When butterfly counts fluctuated between categories along images of a time series, we consistently tried to apply the lower estimate as the starting point. Labelers also recorded whether cells received direct sunlight, and classified cells as receiving direct sunlight when branches or butterflies exhibited additional illumination clearly from direct rather than indirect light. This measurement was recorded only for occupied cells and stored separately. When individual butterflies became difficult to distinguish due to pixel oversaturation, classification required careful attention to subtle shape recognition and contextual awareness about butterfly locations established from previous images in the time series. Labelers received ongoing feedback throughout the classification process. All classifications underwent review (KN) for common errors including mislabeled cells, incorrect category assignments, and inconsistent counting-criteria application. Direct communication of corrections to all labelers ensured consistent protocol application.

\subsubsection{Butterfly Index Calculation}

We calculated a butterfly index (BI) for each frame in a deployment time series:
\begin{equation}
\text{Butterfly index} = \sum_{i} \rho_{i} \times C_{i}
\end{equation}
where $\rho_{i}$ represents the number of cells in category $i$, and $C_{i}$ represents the protocol based count estimate for that category. We used minimum (conservative) category values ($C_{1} = 1$ for category 1--9, $C_{2} = 10$ for category 10--99, and $C_{3} = 100$ for category 100--999) rather than midpoint or maximum values to ensure temporal analyses reflected genuine population shifts on an order of magnitude scale.

\subsection{Temperature Data Extraction}

Given that temperature may influence monarch activity and potentially confound wind effects, ambient temperature data were extracted from camera images (30 minute interval) using optical character recognition (OCR). Cameras displayed temperature readings on the image overlay, but values were not accessible through EXIF metadata, necessitating visual extraction. We developed an automated OCR-technology Python script to extract temperature values from images ($\sim$56,000) across all deployments using multiple preprocessing strategies and pattern matching algorithms to accommodate variations in image quality and display characteristics. Following automated extraction, we manually reviewed and corrected edge cases where OCR failed or produced anomalous values. Systematic quality control through visualization of deployment-specific time series enabled identification and correction of erroneous values. This process ensured complete and validated time series temperature values, providing the ambient temperature covariate data required for modeling analyses.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}

\subsubsection{Data Preparation}

We employed a lag-based framework to capture the temporal dynamics of butterfly responses to environmental changes, comparing the butterfly index (BI) between consecutive daytime 30-minute intervals. Observation pairs were constructed by matching BI at time $t$ with BI at time $t - 30$ minutes, applying a $\pm$5-minute tolerance window to accommodate minor temporal variations in image capture. Observation pairs where both time points recorded zero butterflies were excluded as uninformative, reducing the dataset from approximately 2,500 potential pairs to 1,894 across 115 unique deployment-day combinations. The response variable (change in BI between time points) underwent cube root transformation to achieve approximate normality while preserving directional information: $y = \text{sign}(\Delta) \times |\Delta|^{1/3}$, where $\Delta$ represents the difference in BI ($\Delta$BI). Data analysis revealed generally well-behaved distributions except for bimodality in the $\Delta$BI data from SC deployment 8. At SC8, a large butterfly aggregation abruptly declined to near zero without any corresponding changes in the measured environmental variables. This was unlike any other observation in the dataset. Regardless of the bimodality we retained SC8 in the final analysis for three reasons: to maximize sample size, avoid arbitrary data exclusion, and sensitivity analysis showed that the cube root transformation of $\Delta$BI adequately addressed the distributional concerns, and model selection and parameter estimates remaining consistent whether SC8 was included or excluded.

\subsubsection{Variable Selection}

Predictor variables were selected to test specific hypotheses while avoiding multicollinearity. Maximum wind gust speed during each 30-minute interval served as the primary wind metric, while average sustained speed, modal gust, and gust standard deviation were excluded due to high correlation ($r > 0.75$). Other environmental predictors were average temperature between observation pairs, number of butterflies in direct sunlight at the $t$ time point, and to capture diurnal patterns, the time of day was defined as minutes elapsed since the first observation of each day. BI at the previous time point was included as a control variable, enabling distinction between proportional and absolute changes in abundance. When included, this variable tests effects on proportional change; when excluded, models test effects on absolute change.

\subsubsection{Model Framework}

To test for environmental effects on the response variable (BI), the analyses employed generalized additive mixed models (GAMMs) implemented through the mgcv package in R. Model selection compared 52 candidate models using AIC. The candidate set comprised two fundamental frameworks: models including the lag abundance term (24 models) and models excluding it (24 models), with each framework containing null models, single predictor models, additive combinations, two- and three-way interactions, and models incorporating smooth terms for non-linear relationships. Random effects accounted for variation at three hierarchical levels: deployment location, observer, and deployment-day. Temporal autocorrelation within days was addressed using a first-order autoregressive (AR1) correlation structure grouped by deployment-day. All models were fitted using REML estimation.

To specifically test for threshold effects of the Disruptive Wind Hypothesis' proposed 2~m/s disruptive wind speed, we conducted a threshold analysis using an alternative wind metric. We repeated the entire model selection process with a threshold-based predictor: the count of minutes within each 30-minute period where wind gusts equaled or exceeded 2~m/s. This variable ranged from 0 to 30 minutes and was tested using the same 52 model structures (above), allowing direct comparison of continuous versus threshold-based wind effects.

\subsubsection{Model Validation}

Model assumptions were verified through standard residual diagnostics including examination of residual distributions, fitted versus residual plots, and quantile-quantile plots. Convergence was confirmed for all candidate models in both the continuous and threshold wind disruption analyses. Model performance and predictor significance were evaluated through AIC comparison, with models differing by less than 2 AIC units considered equivalent.

\subsubsection{Statistical Power Analysis}

We evaluated the statistical power to detect wind effects (if present) by conducting a simulation-based power analysis, thereby assessing our ability to detect various effect sizes given 1,894 paired observations. We simulated 200 datasets from the best-fitting model (which excluded wind effects) and artificially introduced wind effects of known magnitude ranging from 0.05 to 0.20 standard deviations of the response variable. For each effect size, we refitted models including wind terms to determine the proportion of simulations where the artificial effect was detected as statistically significant ($\alpha = 0.05$). This simulation approach accounts for the complexity of our GAMM framework and hierarchical data structure, providing robust estimates of statistical power for detecting wind effects across a range of biologically plausible magnitudes.

\subsubsection{Dynamic Window Analysis}

The response time expected for changes in the response variable is technically unknown. The Disruptive Wind Hypothesis does not address response time(s), but we surmise they should be a function of environmental conditions and butterfly overwintering biology. In the analyses outlined above (30-minute), we assumed that the response time could be at that level of resolution (30-minutes) under flight temperature conditions or after shivering. But it could be longer. For example, a response could occur over the course of one day: a daytime disruptive wind event, later followed by a threshold temperature that might support a behavioral response. Or an evening/overnight disruptive wind event might lead to a response the following day. Furthermore, monarchs may make clustering decisions on longer temporal horizons by not responding to immediate environmental conditions. To examine whether day-to-day changes in BI depend on cumulative weather exposure and are not just dependent on changes over a 30-minute interval, we conducted a complementary analysis using dynamic temporal windows. We tested two longer time windows to examine the robustness of our findings based on the 30-minute response window. First, a ``24-hour'' window provided a standardized comparison, extending exactly 24 hours from the previous day's maximum BI (i.e., allowing for the maximum possible response). Second, a biologically-aligned ``Next Day Window'' spanning from the previous day's maximum BI to the current day's last observation, approximating functional sunset on the second day when roosting decisions would have a second chance to be finalized. The Next Day Window interval might thus allow for roosting decisions that could not be made the previous evening because of temperatures below flight threshold. This window varied in duration (mean = 29.6 hours) capturing the exposure period of a peak aggregation through two potential opportunities for a subsequent roosting decision.

Daily aggregate metrics were computed for each deployment-day combination, including maximum BI, 95th percentile BI, and mean of the top three BIs. We retained only days with 15--25 daytime photographs to ensure adequate temporal coverage, then constructed consecutive day pairs separated by exactly one day. Pairs where both days had a maximum BI of zero were excluded as uninformative. The response variable, defined as the change in maximum daily BI over the designated dynamic window, underwent signed square root transformation to achieve approximate normality while preserving directional information.

Weather metric variable selection for the dynamic window analyses began with 19 candidate predictors. Candidates included: spanning baseline BI metrics, temperature summaries (minimum, maximum, and mean values, plus cumulative metrics such as degree-hours above 15$^{\circ}$C), wind exposure measures (average sustained speed, maximum gust, cumulative gust exposure, and time above the proposed 2~m/s threshold), and cumulative sun exposure (the cumulative BIs observed in direct sunlight across all frames within the window). The sun exposure candidate predictor integrated both exposure duration and instantaneous abundance. We conducted correlation analysis to identify multicollinearity within predictor families. Based on this analysis, we selected predictors that minimized redundancy while maintaining interpretability and adequate degrees of freedom relative to sample size. We selected maximum wind gust as the primary wind metric because it captured the variance of all wind variables in a single interpretable measure, showed high correlation with sustained speed, gust summation, and threshold exceedance metrics, and duplicated our selection for the 30-minute analysis. Mode wind gust was excluded because it contained too few unique values, causing model convergence issues. We excluded the candidate predictors mean temperature and degree-hours above 15$^{\circ}$C to reduce multicollinearity. We selected two non-overlapping candidate predictors for temperature (minimum and maximum). The cumulative direct sun exposure variable was retained. For the Next Day Window analysis, we included lag duration (hours) as a control variable to account for varying window lengths. Overall, we required 95\% data completeness across temperature, wind, and daylight observations, calculated as the geometric mean of coverage ratios.

Model selection followed the same information-theoretic approach as the 30-minute analysis. We evaluated 78 candidate models per window type using generalized additive mixed models with deployment random intercepts and AR(1) temporal correlation structures. The candidate set included null models, single predictors, additive combinations, and models with smooth interaction terms. To address potential overfitting given the reduced sample size ($n$ = 94--96 pairs), we applied conservative thresholds for the ratio of effective degrees of freedom to sample size and conducted leave-one-deployment-out cross-validation on top-performing models.

\subsection{Descriptive Statistics}

Environmental conditions varied substantially across the 80-day monitoring period at SC and UDMH during the 2023--2024 overwintering season. The dataset comprised 2,028 unique observations collected at 30-minute intervals during daylight hours (06:00--17:00) from November 17, 2023, to February 4, 2024, totaling 1,014 observation hours across 115 unique deployment-days and producing approximately 56,000 images. Of these, 1,894 formed consecutive 30-minute pairs used in the primary analysis.

Wind speeds ranged from complete calm to moderately strong, with mean = 2.2 $\pm$ 1.4~m/s, median = 2.2~m/s, and maximum gust speeds reaching 12.4~m/s. The interquartile range of 1.3--3.0~m/s indicated that most observations occurred under relatively mild wind conditions. Temperatures showed considerable variation throughout the monitoring period, ranging from 3.0 to 30.0$^{\circ}$C, with mean = 14.5 $\pm$ 3.9$^{\circ}$C, median = 14.0$^{\circ}$C, and an interquartile range of 12.0--17.0$^{\circ}$C typical of California coastal winter conditions. Direct solar exposure of clustering monarchs occurred in 30.2\% of observations ($n$ = 612), with butterflies actively basking when present in sunlight. When direct sunlight was available, an average of 16.8 individual butterflies occupied sunlit positions (range: 1--295), though most observations recorded no butterflies in direct sun, with the distribution heavily skewed toward low or zero values.

Collectively, butterfly abundance ranged from 0 to 950 individuals per image, with mean of 80.9 $\pm$ 101.4 and median of 36 butterflies. The wide interquartile range (8--118) reflected substantial variation in cluster sizes (BI). Zero-count observations, representing either the beginning of cluster formation or cluster dissolution, comprised 3.5\% of the dataset ($n$ = 71).

Monarch abundance (BI) exhibited high variability across site and time. BIs varied noticeably among the deployment locations, with site UDMH2 recording the largest aggregation at 950 monarchs, while mean abundances per deployment ranged from 0.5 at SC9 to 382.5 at UDMH2. Eight deployments observed maximum BI exceeding 100 butterflies, with mean maximum BI across sites reaching 336.7 individuals. This variation in BI across deployments captured the heterogeneous distribution of monarchs. Peak activity occurred at 16:00 hours (196 observations), corresponding with afternoon warming periods when monarchs typically exhibit increased movement (see below).

\subsection{Linear Regression Between Maximum Wind Speed and Cluster Dynamics}

We tested whether wind speed alone could explain changes in monarch cluster size ($\Delta$BI). The 30-minute window (Figure~\ref{fig:wind_linear_30min}) revealed no statistically significant relationship between maximum wind speed and $\Delta$BI ($\beta$ = 1.19 butterflies per m/s, SE = 0.66, $p$ = 0.073, $n$ = 1,894), with a negligible correlation ($r$ = 0.041, $R^{2}$ = 0.002) explaining only 0.17\% of the variance in $\Delta$BI. Data points scattered uniformly across all wind speeds from calm conditions to 12.4~m/s. Additionally, a heuristic test of the Disruptive Wind Hypothesis 2~m/s behavioral threshold (Figure~\ref{fig:wind_linear_30min}) showed no apparent demarcation in butterfly responses, with similar variance in $\Delta$BI both above and below this threshold. The Next Day Window analyses (Figure~\ref{fig:wind_linear_sunset}) similarly revealed no significant relationship between maximum wind exposure and $\Delta$BI ($\beta$ = 5.34 butterflies per m/s, SE = 4.63, $p$ = 0.252, $n$ = 101). The correlation remained weak ($r$ = 0.115, $R^{2}$ = 0.013), with wind speed explaining only 1.3\% of the variance in $\Delta$BI between consecutive days. All observation periods in the Next Day Window analysis experienced maximum wind speeds exceeding the Disruptive Wind Hypothesis 2~m/s behavioral threshold with no apparent response (i.e., winds $>$ 2~m/s do not seem to have been avoided). Likewise, despite consistent exposure to winds that exceeded the threshold, no clear heuristic pattern emerged linking wind intensity to subsequent roost abandonment or growth.

These null linear regression relationships indicated that the Disruptive Wind Hypothesis alone cannot predict monarch clustering behavior, thus necessitating examination of more complex environmental interactions and conditional relationships that may modulate wind effects.

\subsection{Threshold Wind Disruption Analysis}

To determine whether wind is best characterized as a continuous variable or a binary disruption threshold, we repeated the entire model selection process using ``minutes above 2~m/s'' as the wind predictor. We do not summarize the interaction partial effects here, but present the complete threshold analysis results in Appendix~\ref{app:threshold_models}. We note that parameter estimation was not the objective of this comparison; rather, hypothesis testing was the objective. The best threshold model (T50) captured only 55.4\% of model weight compared to 40.4\% for a model without any wind term ($\Delta$AIC = 0.63), and showed comparable explanatory power (adjusted $R^{2}$ = 0.061) to the continuous wind model (adjusted $R^{2}$ = 0.064). We find that the threshold approach does not improve the characterization of wind effects. While the threshold approach (2~m/s) may not optimally characterize wind effects, the interaction between wind (as a continuous or discrete variable) and solar exposure is a better characterization.

\subsection{Next Day Window and Site Fidelity Analysis}

As noted, in the 30-minute interval analysis we assume butterflies can respond to unfavorable conditions over that interval. This assumption may not hold when temperatures fall below the flight threshold, or when shivering is too physiologically expensive, or when clustering decisions occur on a longer temporal horizon. Meaning there could be an apparent lag in response ($\Delta$BI). To address these limitations, we examined 96 consecutive-day pairs using a variable time interval spanning from the previous day's maximum BI to the consecutive day's last observation at sunset (mean duration = 29.6 hours). This approach tests whether cumulative weather exposure over a longer interval is what actually influences $\Delta$BI. Inadvertently, it is also a test of site fidelity decisions over the course of two evenings: if butterflies leave a cluster, they could choose to return or not by the following day's sunset. Returning would boost or maintain the maximum BI at sunset; not returning would lower it. A secondary analysis (below) using a standard 24-hour window ($n$ = 94 pairs) provided a sensitivity test of our findings.

Daily maximum BI per cluster ranged from 0 to 770 butterflies (mean = 134.7 $\pm$ 138.1), with Next Day Window changes in maximum BI ranging from losses of 376 butterflies to gains of 464 butterflies (mean change = $-$10.5 $\pm$ 111.6). Maximum interval wind gusts ranged from 2.0 to 12.8~m/s (mean = 4.5 $\pm$ 1.8~m/s). Cumulative direct sun exposure varied from 0 to 1,122 butterflies observed per interval (mean = 139.8 $\pm$ 206.9), reflecting diverse thermal exposure conditions across monitoring days.

\section{Discussion}
%% ------------------------------------------------------------------

For over three decades, conservation practice has operated under the assumption that wind speeds exceeding 2~m/s force butterflies to abandon their roosts, either by physically dislodging them or triggering behavioral avoidance \cite{leongRestorationOverwinteringGrove1999,leongEvaluationManagementCalifornia2016,xercessocietyStateMonarchOverwintering2016,jepsenProtectingCaliforniasButterfly2017,peltonMonarchButterflyOverwintering2020,weissRecommendationsRestorationMonarch2008,althouse&meadeinc.EllwoodMesaSperling2023}. Our empirical assessment of the Disruptive Wind Hypothesis tested predictions made under this hypothesis: a) that wind would emerge as a significant predictor of change in overwintering cluster size, b) that cluster size would decline at roosts experiencing winds exceeding 2~m/s, c) that cluster size decline would be proportional to wind speeds increases over 2~m/s, d) and that disruptive winds decrease cluster site fidelity manifested as cluster size reductions over consecutive days. Our findings reject the predictions of the Disruptive Wind Hypothesis. Though there does appear to be a ``wind effect,'' it is not at 2~m/s; it is not a threshold; it is not a sole effect; it exists only in combination with direct sunlight. Therefore, we find that maximum wind speed and exposure to direct sunlight interact and scale in complex ways, with other factors, including time of day, previous overwintering cluster size, and potentially temperature also having predictive power \cite{ibsenDensityDependenceHabitat2026}.

\subsection{Evidence Against Wind Disruption}

We tested the most basic prediction of the Disruptive Wind Hypothesis, that increasing wind speed should produce decreasing cluster sizes. We found essentially no correlation at either temporal scale (30-minute $R^{2}$ = 0.002, $n$ = 1,894; two sunset cycles $R^{2}$ = 0.013, $n$ = 96) across wind speeds ranging from calm to six times the proposed threshold.

Modeling revealed that wind alone showed no disruptive effect. Instead, monarch responses were a complex interaction of wind and solar exposure. This interaction showed significance at $p < 0.001$ in both the 30-minute and two sunset cycle analyses. Indeed, wind effects depended entirely on light conditions. For butterflies in shade, wind speed (0 to 12.4~m/s) had no effect on cluster dynamics. Yet once butterflies occurred in sunlit positions, relationships became complex and conditional.

Strikingly, every observation period over the two sunset cycle experienced maximum wind speeds that exceeded 2~m/s (range: 2.0--12.8~m/s), yet monarch clusters persisted throughout the 78-day study period. If the Disruptive Wind Hypothesis were correct, we should have observed either mass departures when temperatures permitted flight or butterflies physically dislodged and littering the ground when too cold to fly \cite{leongRestorationOverwinteringGrove1999}. We observed neither.

\subsection{Thermoregulation as an Alternative Explanation}

Empirical testing has gradually rejected the Microclimate Hypothesis' prediction that overwintering western monarch butterflies have a uniform set of microclimate requirements \cite{fisherMultiScaleSpeciesDistribution2024}
\cite{sanieeHierarchyScaleInfluence2022,ibsenDensityDependenceHabitat2026}
. Rejection of the Microclimate Hypothesis has pointed to the alternative hypothesis that monarch overwintering habitat utilization is driven by physiological and energetic constraints interacting with local conditions \cite{fisherMultiScaleSpeciesDistribution2024}
\cite{sanieeHierarchyScaleInfluence2022}. None of this work has specifically tested physiological and energetic constraints or the thermic and convective environment. But all of it points to the physiological and energetic constraint hypothesis proposed by Chaplin and Wells \cite{chaplinEnergyReservesMetabolic1982} and Masters et al.\ \cite{mastersMonarchButterflyDanaus1988}. Here we briefly summarize salient features of the physiological and energetic constraint hypothesis, while acknowledging that we did not attempt to explore this parameter space either.

Masters et al.\ \cite{mastersMonarchButterflyDanaus1988} demonstrated that cold monarchs actively seek sun. The seeking allows warming from below flight threshold (12.7$^{\circ}$C) to well above it within one minute. Solar basking is effective from ambient temperatures as low as 9$^{\circ}$C. This is inferred to provide an energetic advantage over the alternative warming mechanism, as Kammer \cite{kammerThoracicTemperatureShivering1970} showed that shivering requires 25 to 31 times more energy than resting. However, Masters et al.\ \cite{mastersMonarchButterflyDanaus1988} also found that monarchs avoid sun when thoracic temperatures approach 30$^{\circ}$C. They conclude that monarchs depart sunny cluster sites to avoid overheating and excessive lipid depletion. These patterns, and the inferences that result from them, are what we refer to as the physiological and energetic constraint hypothesis. Here, we outline how the present work also points to the physiological and energetic constraint hypothesis, or possibly a physiological and energetic facilitation hypothesis.

Through modeling we find that time since sunrise ($F$ = 9.85, $p < 0.001$) and a diurnal pattern with morning departures (reductions in BI) and afternoon returns (increases in BI) (Figure~\ref{fig:partial_effects_30min}) are significant explanatory variables. This captures a diurnal pattern that has been consistently documented at California overwintering sites \cite{tuskesOverwinteringEcologyMonarch1978,chaplinEnergyReservesMetabolic1982}. Temperature showed a marginally non-significant effect herein ($p$ = 0.057) (Figure~\ref{fig:partial_effects_30min}), but a significant one in Saniee and Villablanca \cite{sanieeHierarchyScaleInfluence2022}. So, if we allow ourselves to interpret the pattern between BI and temperature, we see departures (reductions in BI) are associated with a short time since sunrise and a rise from the lowest temperatures sampled (Figure~\ref{fig:partial_effects_30min}).

Physiologically, what would we predict from a combination of air temperature and sun exposure? We imagine an exclusive effect of heat transfer from air (that avoids shivering) when direct sun exposure is not an option (shade or cloudy). Conversely, we imagine that when clusters are exposed to the morning sun and a clear sky, for a sufficient duration (i.e., one minute), the effect of low air temperatures would be irrelevant. Potentially, the duration of sun exposure required to reach flight temperature could be a function of ambient air temperature. But, overall, we would predict direct sun exposure to be a more significant term than air temperature. Direct sunlight changes thermal status much faster \cite{mastersMonarchButterflyDanaus1988}, could cause a larger absolute change in thermal state \cite{barkerEffectPhotoperiodTemperature1976}, and could even cause thoracic temperature to exceed ambient temperature \cite{mastersMonarchButterflyDanaus1988}. In summary, air temperature alone could define thoracic temperature (under shady conditions), could be preempted by direct sun (especially under cold sunny conditions), or could interact with sun exposure to define behavioral responses under intermediate conditions.

On the cooling side of the equation, there seems to be an obvious potential for a wind effect. The observed increase in cluster size at intermediate wind and sun levels (Figure~\ref{fig:interaction_wind_sun_30min}) could reflect a thermoregulatory balance where wind moderates the heating effects of sun exposure. Masters et al.\ \cite{mastersMonarchButterflyDanaus1988} observed that monarchs dissipate heat while gliding via airflow across their bodies. Similarly, wind could provide passive convective cooling to stationary butterflies. Riddell and Porter \cite{riddellWindNicheThermal2025} found that wind-driven convective cooling interacts strongly with solar radiation effects in terrestrial organisms. These hypothetical wind, sun, and temperature interactions are consistent with the literature and with a physiological and energetic constraint hypothesis. This hypothesis then leads to a number of interesting predictions.

This potential three-way interaction leads to one prediction that is counterintuitive under a Disruptive Wind Hypothesis. Butterflies may seek windier roosts. By not avoiding wind but instead seeking it they would be able to dissipate heat. This cooling mechanism might allow butterflies to utilize warmer locations in a grove, or to persist at a cluster site longer than they otherwise could. Wind could effectively offset solar heat gain or ambient heat load cancelling the need for departure. Interestingly, the absence of solar heat gain or ambient heat load would lead to another prediction that is counterintuitive under a Disruptive Wind Hypothesis. Monarchs may not avoid winds. Shaded butterflies would experience no solar heat gain, making wind-driven cooling unnecessary and potentially explaining the absence of wind effects in shade (Figure~\ref{fig:interaction_wind_sun_30min}). Finally, if our interpretation is correct, then we predict that under the warmest ambient conditions overwintering butterflies should be observed occupying shadier and windier sites in order to dissipate heat and conserve lipids.

If we combine convective wind dynamics, air temperature, direct sun exposure, shading and cloudiness, to the determination of thoracic temperature and lipid thermodynamics, then we begin to consider the parameters of a physiological and energetic constraint model that should better explain cluster dynamics than a Disruptive Wind model. We propose this as the next line of inquiry for understanding the dynamics of overwinter clustering relative to local environmental factors.

\subsection{Limitations and Context}

Several factors shape the interpretation of our findings. First, our data come from a single overwintering season (2023--2024) when the monarch population was low \cite{xercessocietyWesternMonarchButterfly2025,ibsenDensityDependenceHabitat2026}
. The 2024--2025 season saw near-complete absence of monarchs at our study sites, coinciding with the second-lowest overwintering population on record \cite{xercessocietyWesternMonarchThanksgiving2025}. This absence prevented temporal replication but underscored the urgency of understanding overwintering ecology with the data we have.

Second, though we did not observe it, we do not dispute that wind can dislodge butterflies from a cluster. The wind disruption hypothesis was developed during an era of massive aggregations containing hundreds of thousands of individuals, where many butterflies attached only to other butterflies in multi-layered formations \cite{leongMicroenvironmentalFactorsAssociated1990,browerMonarchButterflyClusters2008}. In contrast, our study observed relatively small clusters where a large proportion of clustering butterflies could maintain direct contact with eucalyptus substrates. If substrate attachment provides greater wind resistance than butterfly-to-butterfly attachment, wind might affect these different clustering configurations differently. It is thus possible that the Disruptive Wind Hypothesis may be more accurate for the extreme densities of past decades but less relevant to today's smaller populations. Alternatively, the fact that we did not see dislodged butterflies even at wind speeds above 12~m/s when branches were swinging wildly, leads us to suspect that there may be an unrecognized interaction between wind and near freezing temperature that facilitates dislodgement.

Finally, our models explained relatively little variance overall (30-minute 6.4\%; two sunset cycles 39.7\%), reflecting both the complexity of butterfly behavior and our focus on testing specific hypotheses rather than comprehensively explaining movement patterns. Regardless, the Wind Disruption Hypothesis predicts substantial observable impacts, not subtle ones. Our analyses achieved 87.5\% power to detect moderate effects and 98.5\% power for large effects. Our failure to detect effects, despite adequate power, validated methodology, and successful identification of other environmental predictors and interaction terms, provides strong evidence against the hypothesis rather than merely absence of evidence.

\subsection{Unexplained Factors of Variation, Resolution and Replication}

Our best models explained 39.7\% of variance and suggested the existence of unaccounted for sources of variance. Here we identify possible sources of variation that we did not address. One is additional time intervals for predicting changes in the response variable ($\Delta$BI). Given a sixfold increase in variance explained between the shorter and longer time frames, we suspect that further increases in time frame would allow for an explanation of more variance. Environmental factors that did explain variance were predominantly thermal in nature. Thermal factors seem to clearly play an important role in organizing clustering behavior. This, and the marginal lack of significance for temperature attributes, suggests that sampling over a greater range of temperatures would provide more information.

Beyond environmental factors, social dynamics may explain additional variance. The strong effect of previous butterfly index on $\Delta$BI (Figure~\ref{fig:partial_effects_30min}) suggests that monarchs exhibit statistically clumped spatial distributions. In spatial statistics, clumped or aggregated distributions are characterized by a higher variance relative to the mean. This pattern is overdispersion, or a contagious distribution, and is common in aposematic overwintering species. A positive feedback mechanism, where initial settlement increases the probability of others joining, creates self-reinforcing (contagious) aggregation patterns independent of immediate environmental conditions \cite{berdahlEmergentSensingComplex2013}. That monarchs form clusters thus suggests that modeling could incorporate clustering itself to help explain variance.

Finally, testing these patterns across the broader overwintering range would establish their generality while allowing the capture of both temporal and spatial variance. Sites with different tree species, latitudes, and especially population densities could reveal whether wind responses vary with clustering configuration or if our findings represent fundamental aspects of western monarch overwintering behavior.

\subsection{Implications for Conservation}

Our findings suggest that wind protection, long considered an essential environmental factor in overwintering monarch conservation, may constrain habitat suitability far less than previously believed. This realization carries important implications for how we understand, manage, and restore overwintering sites. Specifically, we contend that light and wind cannot interact in a positive manner if butterflies are not exposed to both. While butterflies exposed to negative interactions of light and wind would be expected to respond with compensatory avoidance reducing cluster size or cluster site fidelity.

\subsubsection{Expanded Habitat Availability and Population Limitations}

Are we observing limited suitable habitat for overwintering western monarchs? Or, with western monarch populations at historically low levels, is there a limit to the butterflies that can occupy suitable space? The absence of wind disruption despite frequent threshold exceedances indicates that suitable habitat within existing groves may be more extensive than currently recognized, at least at modern temperatures and population sizes. Therefore, groves previously dismissed due to wind exposure might support clusters under larger population conditions, simply because there would be butterflies to occupy them.

\subsubsection{Simplified Management Requirements}

Current management frameworks recommend protecting buffer zones around clustering sites. For example, Althouse \& Meade, Inc.\ and Creekside Science \cite{althouse&meadeinc.EllwoodMesaSperling2023} recommended maintaining trees up to six tree-heights away from clustering locations to ensure adequate wind protection. This translates to protecting all trees within approximately 240 meters for mature trees 40 meters tall. Substantial complexity and potential management hurdles could derive in coastal California where overwintering sites often involve diverse stakeholders and contentious land use decisions. For example, at a major overwintering site in Pacific Grove, California, cluster trees are within meters of a neighborhood fenceline, and a cluster tree extends over the parking lot of a hotel (F.X.V.\ pers.\ obs.)

If wind protection plays a less critical role than assumed, these extensive buffer requirements may not be necessary. Trees adjacent to groves, considered essential for wind breaks, may contribute far less than currently thought. This simplification allows focusing conservation efforts on grove structure rather than on controlling wind in surrounding areas. More importantly, if light and wind (and maybe temperature) are all inter-related through butterfly thermal physiology, then a focus just on wind would be myopic. Likewise, all buffer trees may not have the same role. Saniee and Villablanca \cite{sanieeHierarchyScaleInfluence2022} note that buffer trees to the southeast of a grove reduce storm winds but also early morning winter light.

Nonetheless, buffer trees and surrounding forested areas remain valuable and should be preserved. Wind was not completely ruled out as a factor. Our interaction terms demonstrate that wind plays a conditional role alongside sunlight. Buffer areas also represent potential raw material for future habitat creation: with additional research, these areas could be strategically modified to create new overwintering sites. Furthermore, large mature trees cannot simply be replaced. They require many decades of growth to reach the heights and canopy structure that characterize successful overwintering groves. Losing or prematurely removing these trees is especially consequential because no amount of funding can accelerate this timeline. Prudent conservation therefore favors preserving existing large trees, which can potentially be converted to functional overwintering habitat through informed canopy management.

\subsubsection{Proactive Habitat Creation Through Canopy Management}

The current practice of wind protection employs planting trees and waiting decades for maturation, and also the maintenance of healthy, mature specimens. If monarchs respond primarily to light and thermal patterns rather than strict wind thresholds, strategic canopy modification for light-based management might enable shorter-timeframe interventions.

Real-world examples support this possibility. Weiss et al.\ \cite{weissForestCanopyStructure1991} found that successful overwintering sites maintain consistent canopy openness around 20\%, while unsuccessful sites show greater variability. At Monarch Lane in Los Osos, California, strategic canopy opening resulted in monarchs reoccupying an overwintering site \cite{leongRestorationOverwinteringGrove1999,xercessocietyWesternMonarchButterfly2025}. At Andrew Molera State Park in Big Sur, California, clearing an overgrown grove increased butterfly counts from zero to over one thousand (E.\ Pelton, pers.\ comm.). Conversely, tree planting at Pacific Grove required approximately 15 years of canopy maturation before monarchs returned (S.\ Weiss, pers.\ comm.). These patterns suggest that appropriate canopy structure, including carefully designed openings creating dappled light, can attract butterflies far more quickly than waiting for tree growth alone.

Unoccupied eucalyptus stands or overly dense groves might thus be converted to functional habitat through selective thinning. However, additional research is needed before implementing large-scale canopy modifications, particularly at established overwintering sites. Rather than passive waiting, managers might proactively create suitable conditions by strategically opening existing canopies at appropriate locations (for example previously occupied but currently unused sites), representing a significant departure from previous approaches that focused almost entirely on wind protection and tree growth. This additional approach might be thought of as site restoration and could increase resilience for overwintering monarch butterflies.

\subsubsection{Long-term Optimism Despite Current Uncertainty}

While this study reduces certainty regarding some aspects of overwintering ecology, the long-term trajectory points toward management practices that may be simpler to implement, are grounded in empirical evidence, and potentially are outright effective instead of simply somewhat effective. We conclude that the strict 2~m/s threshold created an overly constraining understanding of habitat requirements, while even suggesting suitable sites were limited. The long-term trajectory suggests managers could be offering more to overwintering monarchs than just wind abatement. Our results indicate that adding access to light and wind should provide significant thermoregulatory and energetic benefits. Likewise, Saniee and Villablanca \cite{sanieeHierarchyScaleInfluence2022} suggest that managing canopy structure to create light and temperature gradients should provide significant thermoregulatory and energetic benefits. But if we can use the Disruptive Wind Hypothesis and the last 30 years as a cautionary tale, then clearly predictions from the physiological and energetic constraint hypothesis (\textit{sensu} Chaplin and Wells \cite{chaplinEnergyReservesMetabolic1982} and Masters et al.\ \cite{mastersMonarchButterflyDanaus1988}) should be rigorously tested.


%% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusions}
%% ------------------------------------------------------------------

Our direct test of the wind disruption hypothesis found no evidence that wind speeds above 2~m/s force overwintering monarchs to abandon their clusters. Every observation in our two sunset cycle analysis exceeded this threshold, yet clusters persisted. Linear regression analyses showed no relationship between wind and cluster changes. Model selection consistently identified other factors as more important. These multiple lines of evidence converge on a clear conclusion. Wind alone does not disrupt overwintering monarch butterflies as has been assumed for over three decades.

Instead, our results suggest that thermal factors may play a more important role in clustering dynamics than previously recognized. Monarchs responded strongly to direct sunlight which could directly influence body temperature. The unexpected wind-light interaction, where moderate wind combined with sun exposure sometimes increased cluster sizes, suggests that environmental conditions interact in complex ways that simple threshold-based management metrics cannot capture.

These findings arrive at a critical moment for monarch conservation. With western populations at historic lows, every assumption about habitat requirements deserves scrutiny. While questions remain about the precise mechanisms, our findings suggest that exploring a physiological and energetic constraint model will be useful, and demonstrate that current management guidelines based on wind speed thresholds are not supported by empirical evidence. Moving forward, conservation efforts should prioritize maintaining existing overwintering groves and restoring unoccupied sites or creating new sites. As we face the challenge of preserving overwintering habitat for a declining population, evidence-based understanding of monarch ecology becomes not just scientifically important, but essential for preserving the remarkable phenomenon of monarch migration in western North America.

\textbf{Model Structure.}

\begin{itemize}
